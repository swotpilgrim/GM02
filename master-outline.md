# The Gulf Shore Drownings - Master Outline

## CHAPTER 1: ARRIVAL
**Act:** 1
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Marcus arrives at the Sandpiper Beach Resort for his third summer as head lifeguard, finding the place more faded than he remembers. He reconnects with Carter at the front desk and meets new hires Jillian (equipment rental) and Lisa (waitress). Bobby, the maintenance guy, is cold to the newcomers. The resort owner, Mr. Kowalski, nervously mentions "some trouble" earlier in the season but brushes it off. Marcus sets up his lifeguard tower, notes calm gulf conditions, perfect visibility. The beach is beautiful but oddly empty for early June. That evening the group has drinks at the tiki bar, establishing dynamics—Carter's charm, Jillian's curiosity, Bobby's resentment, Lisa's quiet observation. Marcus feels good about the summer ahead. Pan out to show someone watching them from a boat offshore.

**Tone:** Nostalgic summer opening, establishing normalcy before horror, subtle wrongness

**Word count: 139**

## CHAPTER 2: THE FIRST DROWNING
**Act:** 1
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Day three. Marcus is on duty when a tourist starts waving frantically—her husband went under and didn't come up. Marcus responds immediately, diving into the calm, clear water. He finds the body in eight feet of water, face down. Performs CPR on the beach while Carter calls for ambulance. The man is dead. Marcus is shaken—this shouldn't have happened. Victim was in waist-deep water, calm conditions, described as a good swimmer by his wife. Sheriff's deputy takes statements, rules it accidental drowning, possibly heart attack or cramp. Marcus examines the body before the ambulance takes it—notices strange bruising on the ankles but deputy dismisses it. That night Marcus can't sleep, replaying the rescue. Something felt wrong. The water was too calm. The body was too deep for where the wife said he'd been.

**Tone:** Visceral emergency response, first seeds of wrongness, Marcus's professional instincts trigger

**Word count: 146**

## CHAPTER 3: THE SECOND DROWNING
**Act:** 1
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Five days later, another drowning. This time Marcus isn't on duty—Jillian discovers the body during her morning equipment check. Young woman, college age, face-down in shallow water near the pier. Same impossible circumstances—strong swimmer according to friends, calm water, no explanation. Panic spreads among tourists. Several families check out early. Mr. Kowalski is furious, worried about the resort's reputation. Sheriff returns, interviews everyone, still ruling accidents. At dinner that night, Lisa mentions the local legend quietly—"La Llorona del Golfo," the weeping woman who drowned herself in 1957 and pulls swimmers under. Bobby scoffs loudly, calls it "tourist bullshit." But Carter notices Bobby won't make eye contact. Jillian is curious about the legend from an anthropological perspective. Marcus remains skeptical but notes the identical circumstances bothering him.

**Tone:** Escalation, tourist panic, folklore introduced, group starting to pay attention

## CHAPTER 4: GHOST STORIES

## CHAPTER 5: IMPOSSIBLE CIRCUMSTANCES
**Act:** 1
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Marcus is on tower duty, mid-afternoon, perfect visibility. Fewer than a dozen people on the beach. He's watching a family in the water—parents and teen son. The father goes under. Marcus sees it happen. He's in the water in seconds, reaches the spot in under thirty seconds. The man is already eight feet down, not struggling, sinking. Marcus brings him up, tries CPR. Dead. This is Marcus's breaking point—he was watching, the water was clear as glass, the man just went under like something pulled him. No splash, no panic, just down. Sheriff arrives, same routine. Marcus insists this wasn't accidental—something dragged him down. Deputy is dismissive: "Son, people drown. It happens." Marcus examines the body again before it's taken—identical bruising on both ankles, oval patterns. He takes a Polaroid discretely. That night he can't stop looking at the photo. Those are hand marks.

**Tone:** Professional breaking point, Marcus's certainty something is hunting swimmers, visceral dread

## CHAPTER 6: TAKING IT SERIOUSLY
**Act:** 1
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Marcus confronts him after—these aren't accidents. Kowalski threatens to fire him if he spreads panic. Marcus finds the others at The Gulfview. He shares the Polaroid of the bruising, his conviction these are murders. Carter thinks he's reaching—people die, drownings happen. Jillian studies the photo, sees what Marcus sees: those could be grip marks. Bobby is nervous, wants no part of investigating. Lisa is quiet but finally speaks: "My grandmother drowned here. 1959. They said accident. But she was a strong swimmer. Grew up on these waters." This shifts everything. Each person's motivation for investigating emerges: Marcus's professional duty, Jillian's scientific curiosity, Carter's boredom turning to genuine intrigue, Lisa's need for answers, Bobby's conflicted loyalty. They agree to investigate quietly.

## CHAPTER 7: LISA'S STORY
**Act:** 1
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Lisa opens up about her grandmother, Marie Thibodeaux, who drowned in 1959 when Lisa's mother was a baby. Body found in shallow water, calm day, no explanation. Marie's mother (Lisa's great-grandmother) insisted Marie would never have drowned—she was "water-born," practically lived in the gulf. The family never believed the official story but had no evidence of foul play. Lisa's great-grandmother died believing her daughter was murdered, claiming she heard crying from the gulf the night Marie died. Lisa shares a photo of Marie—young, smiling, on the same beach. Marcus sees the haunted look in Lisa's eyes and commits fully to solving this. The group decides to approach it methodically: Jillian will try to examine the recent bodies, Carter will use his connections to get official records, Bobby will ask around town carefully, Marcus will research past drownings. End with them shaking hands—an informal pact. They're doing this.

**Tone:** Personal stakes deepen, historical context, group solidifies into investigative team

## CHAPTER 8: JILLIAN'S EXAMINATION
**Act:** 2
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Jillian calls in a favor with a med student friend working at the county morgue. She gets access to the most recent body before it's released to family. Under the guise of marine biology research (possible jellyfish study), she photographs and examines the ankle bruising. Her friend confirms: contusions consistent with grip pressure from human hands. Finger-width spacing, oval pattern, significant force. This was manual restraint. Jillian also notes minor abrasions on wrists—victim tried to fight off their attacker. No water in lungs initially (meaning victim was held underwater conscious, fighting). This is definitely homicide. She reports back to the group at their makeshift "headquarters" (Bobby's maintenance shed). Carter is convinced now. Bobby is scared—if there's a killer in Emerald Bay, it's someone local, someone they might know. Marcus asks the critical question: why would someone drown tourists on purpose? What's the motive?

**Tone:** Investigation becomes real, medical evidence confirms human killer, stakes escalate

## CHAPTER 9: CARTER'S BRIBE
**Act:** 2
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Carter drives to the county seat, finds the coroner's office, and bribes a young assistant with $200 (significant money in 1979) for copies of all three autopsy reports. The assistant is nervous but takes the money. Carter returns with the files. The group reviews them in detail: all three victims died of drowning, all three had identical ankle bruising, all three had minor defensive wounds (scratches, abrasions suggesting struggle). Most damning: the coroner noted "evidence suggests manual restraint during drowning" but was pressured by sheriff to rule them accidental—bad for tourism, no witnesses, no suspects. The killer has been operating with official complicity through neglect. They realize they're on their own—local authorities won't help. Jillian creates a timeline: June 3, June 8, June 13. Roughly five days between kills. If the pattern holds, the next drowning could be in two days.

## CHAPTER 11: VICTIM PROFILES

The pattern goes back decades. Lisa finds a 1959 obituary: her grandmother Marie, drowned. Same year, same circumstances. Cyrus was operating in those years. The scope of his killings could be enormous.

## CHAPTER 16: THE STAKEOUT

The group stakes out Cyrus's houseboat from a rented boat anchored in the dark marina. After midnight, Cyrus emerges onto the deck. He's carrying something—a doll, waterlogged and ancient.
