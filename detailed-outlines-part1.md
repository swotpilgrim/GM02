## Chapter 1: Arrival

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Marcus, third person limited
**Setting:** Sandpiper Beach Resort, Emerald Bay, Florida
**Time:** Early June 1979, late afternoon

**Purpose:** Establish setting, introduce main characters, create nostalgic summer opening with subtle undercurrent of wrongness before horror begins.

**Beats:**
1. Marcus drives into Emerald Bay in his beat-up Nova, noting the resort looks more faded than last summer—peeling paint, cracked pool tiles, fewer cars in the lot. He's arriving for his third season as head lifeguard.
2. Checks in with Carter at front desk (they worked together last year). Carter introduces new hires: Jillian at equipment rental shack, Lisa working restaurant. Bobby (maintenance) is cold and dismissive to the "college kids."
3. Mr. Kowalski nervously mentions "some trouble earlier in season" but brushes it off when Marcus presses. Marcus sets up his lifeguard tower, notes calm conditions and empty beach—unusual for June.
4. Evening drinks at tiki bar. Group dynamics establish: Carter's charm and wealth, Jillian's analytical curiosity, Bobby's resentment of outsiders, Lisa's quiet observation. Marcus feels optimistic about the summer.
5. Closing image: Pan to someone watching the group from a boat offshore—Cyrus Wade with binoculars, assessing.

**Dialogue Topics:** Small talk about summer plans, Carter joking about resort's decline, Jillian asking about marine life in the gulf, Bobby making dismissive comments about tourists, Lisa mentioning she's local and grew up here.

**Emotional Arc:** Marcus begins confident and relaxed, looking forward to familiar summer routine. Slight unease when Kowalski mentions "trouble" and noticing empty beach, but quickly dismissed as opening-week slowness.

**Key Details:** 1979 period markers (8-track in Marcus's car, transistor radios, no cell phones), vivid beach resort atmosphere, establish Cyrus watching from distance, foreshadow through emptiness and Kowalski's nervousness.

## Chapter 2: The First Drowning

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Marcus, third person limited
**Setting:** Beach and gulf waters, Sandpiper Resort
**Time:** Third day of season, mid-morning

**Purpose:** First drowning establishes the impossible nature of the deaths and triggers Marcus's professional instincts that something is wrong.

**Beats:**
1. Marcus on duty, calm conditions, perfect visibility. Tourist wife frantically waving—husband went under and didn't come back up. Marcus responds immediately, diving into clear water.
2. Finds body in eight feet of water, face down, not moving. Brings him to surface, performs CPR on beach while Carter calls ambulance. Man is dead. Wife hysterical—he was a strong swimmer, just wading.
3. Sheriff's deputy arrives, takes statements, rules accidental drowning (possibly heart attack or cramp). Marcus examines body discretely before ambulance arrives—notices strange bruising on both ankles, oval patterns. Deputy dismisses it when Marcus mentions it.
4. Afternoon: Marcus replays the rescue in his mind. Something wrong about it—body was too deep for where wife said he'd been wading, no signs of distress before going under, those bruises.
5. Night: Marcus can't sleep, staring at ceiling. First drowning on his watch in three years. The wrongness gnaws at him.

**Dialogue Topics:** Wife's panicked description of drowning, Marcus giving CPR commands to bystanders, deputy's routine questions and dismissal of foul play, Carter trying to comfort Marcus that he did everything right, Marcus describing the bruises.

**Emotional Arc:** Marcus goes from routine confidence to visceral emergency response to growing unease. Professional pride is shaken—this shouldn't have happened. Seeds of obsession planted as he can't let go of the wrongness.

**Key Details:** Establish Marcus's lifeguard competence and instincts, the impossible calm conditions, the ankle bruising (key evidence), deputy's dismissiveness, Marcus's photographic memory of the scene haunting him.

## Chapter 3: The Second Drowning

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Jillian, third person limited
**Setting:** Beach near pier, resort grounds
**Time:** Five days after first drowning, early morning

**Purpose:** Second death establishes pattern, introduces folklore explanation, escalates panic, and shifts from isolated incident to investigation mode.

**Beats:**
1. Jillian opening equipment rental shack at dawn, sees something floating near pier. Wades out, discovers body of young woman face-down in shallow water. Screams for help.
2. Marcus and resort staff respond. Woman is dead—college age, spring break group. Same impossible circumstances: friends say she was a strong swimmer, water was calm, no explanation.
3. Tourist panic spreads. Several families pack and check out immediately. Mr. Kowalski furious and panicked—two drownings in a week will destroy the resort. Sheriff returns, more interviews, still ruling accidents.
4. Evening: Group gathers at restaurant after hours. Lisa quietly mentions local legend—"La Llorona del Golfo," the weeping woman who drowned herself in 1957 and pulls disrespectful swimmers under. Bobby scoffs loudly but won't make eye contact.
5. Jillian's marine biology curiosity is triggered—could there be an environmental explanation? Unknown jellyfish? Algae bloom affecting swimmers? Marcus mentions the identical bruising he saw on the first victim.

**Dialogue Topics:** Jillian's shocked reaction discovering body, friends describing victim as experienced swimmer, Kowalski demanding discretion from staff, Lisa telling the legend softly, Bobby's aggressive dismissal hiding nervousness, Marcus and Jillian comparing notes on circumstances.

**Emotional Arc:** Jillian begins scientifically curious but shaken by finding the body. Her analytical mind starts processing it as a puzzle to solve rather than tragedy—coping mechanism. Growing unease as rational explanations fail.

**Key Details:** Victim demographics (young, capable), identical circumstances to first death, resort entering crisis, folklore introduced as alternative explanation, Bobby's suspicious reaction to legend, establish Jillian's scientific approach.

## Chapter 4: Ghost Stories

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Multiple perspectives, ensemble
**Setting:** Beach bonfire after hours
**Time:** Night, two days after second drowning

**Purpose:** Establish group dynamics and individual belief systems (skeptic vs believer), deepen folklore background, create atmospheric dread through campfire-story format.

**Beats:**
1. Group gathers on beach after hours with beers and small fire. Bobby knows where resort owner doesn't patrol. Atmosphere is uneasy—two deaths hanging over them.
2. Jillian explains La Llorona legend from anthropological perspective: the weeping woman, various Gulf Coast versions. Lisa shares local version: Caroline (name), drowned 1957, husband returned from war "changed," she weeps for disrespect to sacred waters and pulls sinners under.
3. Bobby reluctantly admits his grandmother believed it absolutely, claimed to hear crying from the gulf some nights. His stories from childhood—old-timers who wouldn't swim at certain times, drownings that happened too often.
4. Carter mocks supernatural explanation, demands rational cause. Marcus focuses on physical evidence—two victims, same profile, those bruises he saw. Jillian proposes marine biology explanations (jellyfish, toxic algae, unknown organism).
5. Distant sound across dark water—crying, or wind, or birds? Lisa hears it clearly, eyes wide. Bobby tenses. Others uncertain. Moment passes. Agreement to watch the water more carefully.

**Dialogue Topics:** Jillian explaining folklore origins, Lisa's soft storytelling of local version, Bobby's reluctant childhood memories, Carter's skeptical challenges, Marcus redirecting to evidence, debate about natural vs supernatural explanations, reactions to the distant sound.

**Emotional Arc:** Group begins fragmented (different perspectives) and bonds through shared unease. Individual positions establish: Carter's rationalism, Jillian's scientific curiosity, Marcus's evidence-focus, Bobby's conflicted local knowledge, Lisa's quiet belief. Collective dread builds.

**Key Details:** Establish character archetypes (skeptic, believer, etc.), Caroline's name mentioned (connects to villain later), 1957 date (will connect to villain's history), the unexplained crying sound (first supernatural ambiguity), bonfire atmosphere building dread.

## Chapter 5: Impossible Circumstances

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Marcus, third person limited
**Setting:** Beach and lifeguard tower, gulf waters
**Time:** Four days after second drowning, mid-afternoon

**Purpose:** Third drowning happens under Marcus's direct observation, breaking his rational worldview and forcing him to accept something impossible is happening.

**Beats:**
1. Marcus on tower duty, hypervigilant after two drownings. Perfect afternoon—clear sky, calm water, fewer than a dozen people on beach. He's watching a family in the water: parents and teen son.
2. Father goes under. Not a splash, not a struggle—just down, like something pulled him. Marcus sees it happen, is in the water within seconds, reaches the spot in under thirty seconds.
3. Man is already eight feet down, not struggling, sinking. Marcus brings him up, CPR on beach. Dead. This is Marcus's breaking point—he was watching with perfect visibility, the man just went straight down.
4. Sheriff arrives, same routine. Marcus insists this wasn't accidental—something pulled him down. He was watching it happen. Deputy dismissive: "Son, people drown." Marcus examines body again—identical ankle bruising. He takes a Polaroid discretely before body is removed.
5. Night: Marcus obsessing over the Polaroid in his room. Those are hand marks, oval pressure points, finger spacing. Someone grabbed this man's ankles and dragged him down.

**Dialogue Topics:** Marcus's internal dialogue during rescue attempt, his insistent testimony to deputy that something pulled the victim down, deputy's patronizing dismissal, Carter and Jillian trying to comfort Marcus, Marcus describing the bruising pattern as definitively hand-shaped.

**Emotional Arc:** Marcus begins hypervigilant and determined to prevent another drowning. Incident shatters his confidence—he did everything right and victim still died impossibly. Transitions from shaken to grimly certain this is homicide. Professional instinct becomes obsession.

**Key Details:** Establish Marcus witnessed it directly (no room for doubt), the Polaroid evidence (becomes crucial later), hand-shaped bruising confirmed, deputy's dismissal shows authorities won't help, Marcus's certainty this is deliberate murder.

## Chapter 6: Taking It Seriously

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Marcus, third person limited
**Setting:** Resort office, restaurant
**Time:** Day after third drowning, afternoon and evening

**Purpose:** Group commits to investigating despite authority opposition; personal motivations for each character emerge; establish them as autonomous investigators.

**Beats:**
1. Kowalski calls emergency staff meeting. Three drownings in two weeks is catastrophic for business. Instructs everyone to downplay it, reassure guests, avoid word "pattern." Tourism is everything.
2. Marcus confronts Kowalski privately after—these aren't accidents, someone is killing tourists. Kowalski threatens termination if Marcus spreads panic. Marcus realizes authorities/business interests won't help.
3. Marcus finds the group at The Gulfview restaurant after hours. Shows them the Polaroid of ankle bruising, explains his certainty these are murders—someone grabbing ankles underwater, pulling victims down.
4. Group reactions: Carter skeptical (drownings happen, reaching for pattern), Jillian studying photo (could be hand-shaped), Bobby nervous and resistant to investigating, Lisa quiet until she speaks: "My grandmother drowned here. 1959. They said accident. She was a strong swimmer."
5. Lisa's revelation shifts everything. Each person's motivation emerges through conversation: Marcus's duty, Jillian's need to understand, Carter's intrigued despite himself, Lisa's need for answers about her grandmother, Bobby's conflicted loyalty. They agree to investigate quietly without official sanction.

**Dialogue Topics:** Kowalski's businessspeak demands for discretion, Marcus's angry insistence people are being murdered, debate over whether bruising proves human cause, Lisa's story about her grandmother, each person articulating their reason for investigating or resisting, planning how to proceed.

**Emotional Arc:** Marcus goes from angry and stymied by authority to finding allies. Group transitions from separate individuals to united investigators. Lisa's personal connection transforms abstract mystery into moral imperative. Despite fear, they commit.

**Key Details:** Establish institutional opposition (business/police won't help), Lisa's grandmother's 1959 drowning (connects to villain timeline), group formation as investigators, Marcus's Polaroid as first real evidence, decision to act autonomously.

## Chapter 7: Lisa's Story

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Lisa, third person limited
**Setting:** Lisa's mobile home, beach
**Time:** Evening, day after group commits to investigating

**Purpose:** Deepen Lisa's backstory and personal stakes, provide historical context connecting current deaths to decades-old pattern, strengthen group bond through shared purpose.

**Beats:**
1. Lisa invites the group to her mobile home (she lives with her grandmother, three miles inland). Shows them photograph of Marie Thibodeaux, her grandmother who drowned in 1959—young, smiling woman on the same beach.
2. Lisa tells the story: Marie was experienced swimmer, "water-born" according to the family, practically lived in the gulf. Found dead in shallow water on calm day, no explanation. Lisa's great-grandmother insisted Marie would never have drowned, claimed she heard crying from the gulf the night Marie died.
3. Great-grandmother died without answers, convinced Marie was murdered. Lisa's mother grew up without a mother, family carried the grief for decades. Lisa came to work at the resort partially to feel close to where her grandmother died, hoping to understand.
4. Group listens in silence. Marcus sees the haunted look in Lisa's eyes, fully commits to solving this. They discuss dividing investigative tasks: Jillian will try to examine bodies (med school contacts), Carter will get official records (money/connections), Bobby will ask around town carefully, Marcus will research past drownings.
5. Group makes informal pact—handshakes around the small table. They're doing this for Marie, for the recent victims, for everyone who died without answers. Lisa cries, grateful someone finally cares.

**Dialogue Topics:** Lisa describing Marie's life and death, great-grandmother's conviction it was murder and the crying she heard, family's decades of unanswered grief, Lisa's emotional connection to the beach and investigation, group discussing task division and investigative approach.

**Emotional Arc:** Lisa begins hesitant to share family tragedy, becomes emotional as story pours out, ends grateful and determined. Group members individually moved by her story, collective commitment strengthens. Personal stakes now anchor the investigation.

**Key Details:** Marie Thibodeaux 1959 drowning (will appear in villain's journals), great-grandmother heard crying (supernatural element), Lisa's personal motivation now central, group divides tasks (launches Act 2 investigation), photo of Marie (humanizes historical victim).

## Chapter 8: Jillian's Examination

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Jillian, third person limited
**Setting:** County morgue, equipment rental shack
**Time:** Two days after group commits, afternoon

**Purpose:** Confirm murders through medical evidence, transition from speculation to certainty, establish killer's methodology (manual drowning via ankle restraint).

**Beats:**
1. Jillian drives to county morgue, meets med student friend (Tim) who owes her a favor. She pitches it as marine biology research—possible jellyfish attack, needs to examine sting patterns. Tim is skeptical but lets her access most recent victim before family release.
2. Examination: Jillian photographs ankle bruising up close. Friend confirms contusions consistent with grip pressure from human hands, finger-width spacing clearly visible, significant force required. Minor abrasions on victim's wrists—defensive wounds, fighting off attacker.
3. Most damning detail: very little water initially in lungs, suggesting victim was held underwater while conscious and fighting, inhaling water through struggle. Classic manual drowning, not accidental. Tim realizes this is homicide evidence, alarmed. Jillian promises to take it to authorities (lying).
4. Return to resort: Jillian reports findings to group in Bobby's maintenance shed (their unofficial headquarters). Shows Polaroids of bruising and her notes. Medical evidence conclusive: someone is grabbing victims' ankles underwater and holding them down until they drown.
5. Carter is now convinced. Bobby is scared—killer is someone local, someone they might know. Marcus asks key question: why is someone drowning tourists deliberately? What's the motive?

**Dialogue Topics:** Jillian's careful misdirection with Tim about research purpose, Tim's medical analysis of bruising and cause of death, Jillian's clinical presentation of findings to group, group debate about implications (local killer, deliberate selection of victims), speculation about motive.

**Emotional Arc:** Jillian approaches examination clinically (scientist mode), but seeing definitive evidence of murder affects her—these were people, terrified, fighting for life. Reporting to group, she masks emotion with data. Group transitions from theory to grim certainty.

**Key Details:** Medical confirmation of manual drowning, grip marks prove human killer, defensive wounds show victims fought, water-in-lungs detail shows conscious drowning (cruel), establish killer has underwater capability and strength.

## Chapter 9: Carter's Bribe

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Carter, third person limited
**Setting:** County seat, coroner's office, maintenance shed
**Time:** Day after Jillian's examination, morning through afternoon

**Purpose:** Obtain official autopsy reports revealing authority complicity through negligence, establish pattern timeline suggesting next victim imminent, show Carter's resourcefulness.

**Beats:**
1. Carter drives forty miles to county seat in his Trans Am. Coroner's office is typical government building—underfunded, understaffed. He identifies young assistant (early twenties, looks underpaid) and waits for opportunity.
2. Approaches assistant during smoke break. Friendly conversation, establishes rapport. Carter offers $200 cash (significant money in 1979, week's wages) for copies of three drowning autopsy reports. Assistant nervous but tempted. "Just copies, no one gets hurt." Assistant takes the money.
3. Carter returns with manila folder of reports. Group convenes in shed, reviews documents together. All three autopsies note manual restraint bruising, defensive wounds, evidence suggesting homicide. But coroner noted he was "pressured by sheriff to rule accidental—tourism concerns, no witnesses, no suspects."
4. Pattern timeline emerges: June 3, June 8, June 13. Roughly five-day intervals. If pattern continues, next drowning could be June 18—two days away. Jillian charts it visually on maintenance shed wall.
5. Realization: local authorities know these might be murders but chose tourism revenue over investigation. The group is truly on their own. They have less than 48 hours to prevent the next death.

**Dialogue Topics:** Carter's smooth-talking bribe pitch to assistant, group's shocked reading of autopsy details aloud, discussion of coroner's notes about pressure to rule accidents, debate about whether pattern timing is predictive, urgency about preventing next victim.

**Emotional Arc:** Carter begins enjoying playing detective (using wealth as tool, bit of a game), becomes sobered reading autopsy reports—real people died while authorities looked away. Group's anger at official negligence, fear about ticking clock to next victim.

**Key Details:** $200 bribe shows Carter's privilege and resourcefulness, autopsy reports confirm official knowledge and negligence, five-day pattern creates urgency, June 18 deadline establishes ticking clock, group now racing against killer's timeline.

Jillian cross-references victim names with business records (Carter charmed the teenage shop clerk into showing him rental logs). **Dialogue Topics:** Marcus and Jillian analyzing map pattern, revelation about Wade's dive shop proximity, Carter explaining how he got rental logs, Bobby's emotional defense of Cyrus then reluctant acceptance of evidence, group planning surveillance approach.

**Emotional Arc:** Group begins excited by investigative breakthrough (following clues, identifying suspect), transitions to sobering as Bobby's distress reminds them this is real person they're accusing. Bobby feels betrayed—local hero might be monster—trust in community eroding.

## Chapter 11: Victim Profiles

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Jillian and Lisa, alternating scenes
**Setting:** Resort lobby/newspaper archives, various interview locations
**Time:** Day of predicted next drowning, morning

**Purpose:** Establish victim-selection pattern (disrespectful behavior toward gulf), reveal killer's environmental/moral motive, deepen understanding of Cyrus's psychology.

**Beats:**
1. Jillian and Lisa research three victims using newspaper clippings and interviewing hotel guests who knew them. Victim 1 (businessman from Atlanta): multiple witnesses say he complained loudly about "trashy beach," threw beer cans in the water, called the gulf "disgusting."
2. Victim 2 (college student): spring break group, drunk and rowdy, witnesses saw her urinating in shallow water while laughing. Friends confirm she was "wasted" and being obnoxious. Victim 3 (Ohio tourist): caught littering beer bottles on beach, argued with locals who called him out, told them to "mind their own business."
3. Pattern crystalizes: all three exhibited behavior that could be labeled "disrespectful" to the environment/gulf. They weren't random—they were selected. Killer has agenda: punishing people who desecrate the waters.
4. Lisa remembers Bobby mentioning Cyrus yells at tourists who litter, has gotten into public arguments about treating the gulf with respect. Cyrus refers to the gulf as "she" and "her"—personifies it, sees it as sacred.
5. Return to group with findings: This isn't random serial killing, it's environmental extremism or moral punishment. Killer believes he's purifying the waters, removing disrespectful sinners. Cyrus fits the psychological profile perfectly.

**Dialogue Topics:** Witnesses describing victims' disrespectful behavior, Jillian and Lisa discussing the pattern, Lisa recalling Bobby's stories about Cyrus's environmental protectiveness, group analyzing killer psychology (zealot vs psychopath), debate about whether motive makes sense.

**Emotional Arc:** Jillian approaches research analytically but becomes disturbed by implications—killer believes he's righteous. Lisa connects emotionally to environmental respect (locals often do resent tourist disrespect) but horrified by violent response. Group feels closer to understanding killer's mind.

**Key Details:** Victim-selection criteria established (disrespectful behavior), motive revealed (environmental purification), Cyrus's known behavior fits profile, killer sees himself as righteous, distinction between random vs mission-driven killer.

## Chapter 12: The Dive

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Marcus and Bobby, underwater and surface perspectives
**Setting:** Gulf waters, three drowning sites, dive shop
**Time:** Afternoon of predicted next drowning

**Purpose:** Discover physical evidence (ambush ropes), confirm killer's methodology and capability, force Bobby to accept Cyrus's guilt.

**Beats:**
1. Marcus convinces Bobby to dive the three drowning sites—looking for physical evidence, understanding the killer's approach. Bobby reluctant but agrees; his local knowledge of underwater geography is essential. They rent equipment from Cyrus's shop first.
2. Interaction with Cyrus: exactly as Bobby described—weathered, soft-spoken, knowledgeable, friendly. Gives Marcus tips on local dive spots, warns about currents that aren't issues. Seems genuinely helpful, even grandfatherly. Makes Marcus doubt for a moment.
3. Underwater at first drowning site: visibility decent, sandy bottom with scattered seagrass. Bobby finds something Marcus would've missed—weighted rope tied to rock formation, hidden in seagrass, easily overlooked. Could be used as anchor point to wait for victim, stay submerged and hidden.
4. Second site has similar rope, concealed. Third site too. These are deliberate—ambush points. Someone with intimate knowledge of underwater terrain set these up. Someone like Cyrus, who's dived these waters for decades.
5. Surfacing: Bobby and Marcus don't speak until they're back at the shed. Bobby looks sick, devastated. "It's him. Cyrus is drowning them." The physical evidence plus the pattern plus the profile—it all points to Cyrus. Bobby's hometown hero is a serial killer.

**Dialogue Topics:** Marcus and Bobby's minimal underwater communication (hand signals), Marcus's polite questions to Cyrus about dive conditions, Bobby identifying the weighted ropes' purpose, Marcus and Bobby's tense post-dive debrief, Bobby's emotional acceptance of Cyrus's guilt.

**Emotional Arc:** Marcus begins methodical (gathering evidence) but seeing Cyrus's friendliness creates cognitive dissonance—monsters don't look like nice old men. Bobby's arc is devastation—finding the ropes forces him to accept the man he trusted is the killer. Betrayal compounds horror.

**Key Details:** Weighted ropes prove premeditation and underwater ambush tactic, Cyrus's diving expertise makes him capable, Bobby's local knowledge crucial to finding evidence, establish Cyrus's surface charm (makes him more dangerous), Bobby's loyalty to town broken.

## Chapter 13: The Interview

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Marcus, third person limited
**Setting:** Wade's dive shop interior
**Time:** Late afternoon, same day as dive

**Purpose:** Establish Cyrus's personality and ideology directly, allow protagonist to interact with villain unknowingly, show Cyrus's unsettling charm and conviction.

**Beats:**
1. Marcus and Carter return to dive shop under pretense: Marcus is "new to the area" (playing dumb), wants Cyrus's advice on safe tourist diving spots. Cyrus welcomes them, makes coffee in the shop's back room, clearly enjoys talking about the gulf.
2. Cyrus speaks lovingly, almost worshipfully, about the gulf—how it's "sacred," "alive," "knows who respects her." Describes tourists as ignorant, disrespectful, not understanding what they're polluting. His passion borders on religious fervor.
3. Mentions his wife Caroline who drowned in 1957. "The gulf took her home. She's part of it now. I hear her sometimes, in the waves." His pale blue eyes have unsettling intensity. He demonstrates equipment obsessively, keeps everything perfectly organized, hums old hymns while working.
4. Marcus mentions the recent drownings carefully. Cyrus's expression doesn't change—calm, matter-of-fact: "Terrible. But the gulf claims the disrespectful. Always has." Spoken like natural law, not tragedy. Slight smile. Marcus's skin crawls.
5. Leaving the shop, Marcus and Carter process in the Trans Am. Carter: "He's either the killer or just a weird old man with strong opinions." Marcus: "He knows more than he's saying. Did you see his face when I mentioned the drownings? No surprise. No concern. Just... satisfaction."

**Dialogue Topics:** Cyrus's philosophical monologues about the gulf's sanctity, stories about his Navy diving days and Caroline, Marcus's careful questions about drownings and water safety, Carter's friendly tourist act, Marcus and Carter's debriefing in the car.

**Emotional Arc:** Marcus begins performing (playing ignorant tourist), becomes increasingly disturbed by Cyrus's intensity and strange affect. Leaves uncertain if they found their killer or an eccentric old man, but instinct says killer. Skin-crawling wrongness beneath surface charm.

**Key Details:** Cyrus's personification of gulf as feminine sacred entity, Caroline mentioned (confirms she's real, died 1957), Cyrus's hymn-humming and obsessive organization (ritualistic behavior), his lack of concern about drownings, Marcus's intuition triggered.

## Chapter 14: Background Check

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Jillian and Lisa, alternating
**Setting:** Local library, microfiche archives
**Time:** Evening after Marcus/Carter interview Cyrus

**Purpose:** Reveal Cyrus's history and origin story, connect Caroline's death to his delusion, establish decades-long timeline of killings, connect Lisa's grandmother to Cyrus.

**Beats:**
1. Jillian and Lisa at library, requesting newspaper archives on microfiche (1979 research technology). Start with Cyrus Wade's background: born 1921 Emerald Bay, Navy diver WWII and Korea (decorated), married Caroline Landry 1955, opened dive shop 1965.
2. Caroline's drowning, 1957, made local headlines: "Local Woman Drowns in Calm Waters - Tragedy Strikes Young Couple." Article details: experienced swimmer, calm conditions, no explanation. Quotes from Cyrus even then show delusion forming: "She's with the gulf now. She's part of it. I hear her sometimes."
3. More findings: 1968 article about Cyrus dismissed from volunteer coast guard for "erratic behavior"—refusing to rescue people he deemed "disrespectful to the waters." Evidence of his mission mentality going back decades.
4. Lisa searches 1959 obituaries, finds her grandmother Marie: "Marie Thibodeaux, 34, drowned June 12, 1959. Survived by husband and infant daughter." Same year range Cyrus was active, same impossible circumstances. Was Cyrus killing even then, two years after Caroline's death?
5. They bring findings to group. Cyrus's delusion has been operating for twenty-plus years. If he killed Lisa's grandmother, and has been selecting "disrespectful" tourists since then, the death toll could be enormous—dozens of murders written off as accidents.

**Dialogue Topics:** Jillian and Lisa discussing microfiche findings, reading historical articles aloud to each other, Lisa's emotional reaction finding her grandmother's obituary, speculation about timeline of Cyrus's killing spree, group discussing historical scope of crimes.

**Emotional Arc:** Jillian remains analytical but increasingly disturbed by scope—this isn't recent, it's institutional. Lisa finds validation (grandmother's death wasn't accident) but also grief (confirms murder). Group feels weight of decades of injustice, anger at all the deaths that went uninvestigated.

**Key Details:** Caroline's death 1957 (origin of Cyrus's delusion), 1968 coast guard incident (evidence of ideology), Marie's death 1959 (connects Lisa personally to timeline), establish 20+ year pattern, confirm Cyrus's delusion is long-standing and acted upon.

## Chapter 15: Bobby's Knowledge

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Bobby, third person limited
**Setting:** Maintenance shed, beach at night
**Time:** Late evening after library research

**Purpose:** Bobby reveals local folklore and childhood encounters with Cyrus, provides insider perspective on community complicity, deepens his character through betrayal and guilt.

**Beats:**
1. Bobby finally opens up about what locals know but don't say. His grandmother told stories about Cyrus—how he'd "talk to the water" late at night after Caroline died, how some thought grief drove him mad, others thought he was "blessed" or "touched by the gulf."
2. Bobby's own memory: age eleven, couldn't sleep, walked to beach at midnight, saw Cyrus on his knees in the sand having full conversation with the waves. Thought it was grief. Cyrus saw him, wasn't angry—smiled and said "She speaks to those who listen." Scared Bobby enough to run home.

5. Lisa comforts Bobby—they're both locals whose home hid something monstrous. Community complicity through willful ignorance isn't their fault, but now they have to act. Bobby commits fully to stopping Cyrus, needs to make up for hometown's failure.

**Dialogue Topics:** Bobby sharing grandmother's stories about Cyrus, describing his midnight beach encounter as a child, discussing local drowning statistics and community explanations, Bobby processing guilt about not suspecting sooner, Lisa reassuring him it's not his fault.

**Emotional Arc:** Bobby begins defensive and ashamed (local who should've known), becomes emotional processing betrayal (man he trusted is killer), ends determined (channeling guilt into action). Group sees Bobby's depth—not just friction character but person carrying community's unspoken knowledge.

**Key Details:** Cyrus's midnight rituals witnessed by locals (long-standing pattern), Bobby's childhood encounter (Cyrus has been delusional for decades), drowning statistics (confirms multi-decade killing spree), local complicity through willful ignorance, Bobby's loyalty shifts from town to group/justice.
